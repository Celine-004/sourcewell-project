# Physical Activity for Diabetes Prevention

The American Diabetes Association (ADA) provides the following recommendation for lifestyle changes for diabetes prevention (derived from the "Lifestyle Behavior Change for Diabetes Prevention" section).The letter at the end of each statement represents the evidence grade:
“3.3 Refer adults with overweight or obesity at high risk of type 2 diabetes, as seen in the Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP), to an intensive lifestyle behavior change program to achieve and maintain a weight reduction of at least 7% of initial body weight through healthy reduced-calorie diet and ≥150 min/week of moderate-intensity physical activity. A” [PMC10725807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10725807/)

“The two major goals of the DPP intensive lifestyle intervention were to achieve and maintain a minimum of 7% weight loss and 150 min of moderate-intensity physical activity per week, such as brisk walking.… The goal for physical activity was selected to approximate at least 700 kcal/week expenditure from physical activity. For ease of translation, this goal was described as at least 150 min of moderate-intensity physical activity per week, similar in intensity to brisk walking. Participants were encouraged to distribute their activity throughout the week with a minimum frequency of three times per week and at least 10 min per session. A maximum of 75 min of strength training could be applied toward the total 150 min/week physical activity goal (11).” [PMC10725807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10725807/)

“Moderate-intensity physical activity, such as brisk walking for 150 min/week, has shown beneficial effects in those with prediabetes (4). … In addition to aerobic activity, a physical activity plan designed to prevent diabetes may include resistance training (11,29,30). Breaking up prolonged sedentary time may also be encouraged, as it is associated with moderately lower postprandial glucose levels (31,32).” [PMC10725807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10725807/)


**Calculator Integration:**
The ADA prescribes “≥150 min/week of moderate-intensity physical activity”[PMC10725807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10725807/) for adults at high risk (Recommendation 3.3, Grade A), and describes distribution and session minimums consistent with DPP. FINDRISC includes a physical activity component (regular activity vs not), so these ADA targets support operational definitions when mapping user responses about habitual activity to the FINDRISC item and counseling thresholds after risk stratification.
